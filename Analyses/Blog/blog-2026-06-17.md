# The Great Unbundling: How Persistent, Self-Improving Loops Are Reshaping Authorship and Sovereignty

Tim Ferriss recently documented a collapse in self-help nonfiction sales 
so steep it cannot be explained by readers simply switching to AI chatbots 
for quick tips. At the same time, the Netherlands is building GPT‑NL, a 
sovereign language model intended to keep Dutch education and cultural 
narratives out of foreign-controlled AI pipelines. One looks like a 
publishing crisis; the other, a geopolitical hedge. Both are surface 
symptoms of the same structural shift: the transition from static, frozen 
artifacts to persistent, self-improving knowledge loops that blur the line 
between training and deployment.

The technical community is converging on looped, adaptive 
architectures—variable-width transformers, fixed-point reasoners, and 
agents that refine their own policies during inference. These systems do 
not merely generate outputs; they adapt and improve with use. That single 
shift is quietly unbundling two entrenched institutions: the book and the 
nation-state’s control over knowledge infrastructure.

## The Architectural Convergence

The traditional transformer operates in two distinct phases: an expensive 
training run produces frozen weights, and inference merely replays that 
fixed knowledge. A new wave of research systematically dismantles this 
divide.

A recent paper on **Variable-Width Transformers** challenges the 
assumption that every layer must share the same width, proposing a 
“><-shaped” profile that allocates more parameters to middle layers and 
fewer to early and late ones. The result is more efficient, 
role-specialized transformers that could run sophisticated reasoning on 
local hardware without hyperscaler budgets.

**Fixed-Point Reasoners** introduce parameter-shared blocks that iterate 
until internal representations converge to a stable fixed point. They 
achieve up to 100× parameter efficiency and enable adaptive depth without 
compounding errors, suggesting a path toward persistent models that refine 
their computation during use rather than remaining frozen between training 
runs.

**Looped World Models** extend the same iterative design to world 
modeling, using parameter-shared transformer blocks to refine predictions 
through repeated computation. Meanwhile, **Learning from the Self-future** 
proposes on-policy self-distillation for diffusion language models, where 
a model treats its own future outputs as a teacher, creating a closed 
improvement loop without external supervision. And **Zone of Proximal 
Policy Optimization** frames the teacher as a prompt provider rather than 
a gradient source, enabling policy improvement through textual feedback. 
If generalized, such techniques could lower the barrier to steering models 
without retraining.

These are not isolated curiosities. They form a coherent design 
philosophy: models that are depth-adaptive, self-improving, and capable of 
learning continuously during interaction. The wall between training and 
inference is crumbling.

The plumbing for this new world is being built by engineers who rarely 
make headlines. When John Carmack publicly described Fabrice Bellard as 
“almost certainly a better overall programmer,” the attention reflected 
Bellard’s particular ethos—the hidden giant who builds foundational, 
auditable, ruthlessly efficient infrastructure. That ethos is exactly what 
looped, sovereign architectures demand. Projects like **cuTile Rust**, 
which brings safe, data-race-free GPU kernels to Rust, hint at an emerging 
ecosystem where reliable, efficient infrastructure matters more than raw 
scale. And **ReproRepo**, a framework that scales reproducibility audits 
through GitHub repository issues, offers a glimpse of how we might verify 
that a continuously evolving model has not drifted into error—a 
quality-assurance mechanism for living knowledge systems.

## The Unbundling of the Book

Tim Ferriss documented a sales decline that cannot be explained by users 
merely asking chatbots for advice. The deeper explanation is that readers 
have abandoned a static format for a different consumption mode entirely. 
A book is a bounded artifact, frozen at publication. An interactive 
intelligence service is an ongoing, personalized dialogue that adapts to 
context and coaches through specific problems.

This is not about AI generating text. It is about the collapse of the 
*format* itself. The book, as a bounded, linear container of expertise, is 
being replaced by a subscription to an evolving expert agent. The author 
does not disappear; the author becomes a knowledge designer—someone who 
structures domain models, designs prompt templates, curates coaching 
loops, and continuously refines the agent’s behavior based on user 
interactions. The product is no longer a manuscript but a living service.

A post titled **“LLMs Will Replace 8-Track Duplication Engineers”** draws 
a useful parallel. The 8-track tape was a physical artifact that captured 
a fixed performance; duplication engineers mass-produced that frozen 
moment. LLMs are doing to static text what digital streaming did to 
physical media—making the duplication of fixed content obsolete while 
enabling infinitely recombinable, on-demand experiences. The self-help 
book is the 8-track of knowledge work.

If this trend continues, the publishing industry’s crisis is only the 
leading edge. Any domain where expertise is packaged as static 
text—textbooks, technical manuals, policy guides, even legal 
commentaries—may undergo the same unbundling. The “book” dies, but the 
“author” may live on as a personalized, AI-augmented service that learns 
from every interaction.

## The Unbundling of the Nation

While the micro-level transformation reshapes authorship, the same 
architectural shift is redrawing geopolitical lines. The Netherlands’ 
**GPT‑NL** project is an explicit attempt to build a sovereign language 
model that aligns with Dutch values and serves Dutch citizens without 
routing every query through a foreign corporation’s data center. The 
motivation is not merely cultural pride; it is a recognition that when 
knowledge becomes a live, AI-mediated flow, control over that flow is a 
strategic asset. A nation without its own living knowledge loop risks 
becoming an “information colony”—dependent on foreign AI for education, 
professional training, and even the framing of its own history.

The economic logic is reinforced by the staggering cost of centralized AI. 
Leaked OpenAI financials revealed a $38.5 billion loss and compute 
spending that hit $34 billion in 2025, an almost eightfold increase in 
losses year over year. That burn rate is unsustainable for any single 
entity, let alone a government hoping to serve millions of citizens. The 
efficiency gains demonstrated by looped architectures—up to 100× parameter 
efficiency in some fixed-point designs—make sovereign models feasible on 
local hardware, enabling decentralized, resilient knowledge 
infrastructures that do not require hyperscaler budgets.

The geopolitical stakes were thrown into sharp relief by the US 
government’s ban on Anthropic models, which a TechCrunch investigation 
revealed was never really about a jailbreak. The incident underscored that 
access to advanced AI can be revoked by political decree, leaving 
dependent nations suddenly cut off from the intelligence layers they have 
woven into their economies. A sovereign loop, running on domestic 
infrastructure and auditable through mechanisms like ReproRepo, is not 
merely a matter of cultural independence—it is a hedge against that kind 
of infrastructural coercion.

A reflective essay titled **“After AI takes everything”** explored the 
post-scarcity implications of advanced AI, but its undercurrent was a 
warning: whoever controls the living knowledge flows controls the reality 
that citizens inhabit. If looped architectures fulfill their promise, the 
question may not be whether nations will build their own models, but 
whether they will build them in time to avoid permanent dependency.

## Forward View

If the looped-architecture paradigm solidifies, several trajectories 
appear plausible—each grounded in the technical signals visible now.

First, the profession of “knowledge designer” will likely emerge as a 
distinct discipline, blending domain expertise with prompt engineering, 
agent orchestration, and continuous evaluation. The **Zone of Proximal 
Policy Optimization** paper, which places the teacher entirely in the 
prompt rather than in gradients, suggests that non-programmers may be able 
to steer and improve living models through natural-language feedback. This 
could lower the barrier to creating personalized expert agents, 
accelerating the unbundling of static nonfiction into interactive services 
across law, medicine, and education.

Second, sovereign AI will probably become a national-security priority on 
par with energy independence. The **GPT‑NL** initiative is an early 
prototype, but the parameter efficiency demonstrated by **Fixed-Point 
Reasoners**—up to 100× in some configurations—indicates that a capable, 
continuously improving model can run on infrastructure a small nation can 
afford. If **ReproRepo**-style reproducibility audits become standard, we 
may see a network of interoperable sovereign loops that share verification 
protocols while maintaining local control: a federated knowledge 
infrastructure that resists both monopolistic centralization and 
information colonialism.

Third, the collapse of the training-inference divide raises profound 
questions about privacy and drift. A model that learns continuously from 
user interactions is also a model that accumulates sensitive behavioral 
data. The **Learning from the Self-future** paper’s on-policy 
self-distillation technique, which uses the model’s own future outputs as 
a teacher, could be adapted to run entirely on-device, keeping 
personalization local. But without careful design, living models could 
become vectors for subtle manipulation, evolving in ways that even their 
designers do not fully understand. The need for auditable, reproducible 
loops will only intensify.

Finally, the hidden giants matter. Fabrice Bellard’s style of 
engineering—compact, auditable, and astonishingly capable software built 
with minimal resources—may become the template for the next generation of 
AI infrastructure. Projects like **cuTile Rust** and **SubQ 1.1 Small** 
hint that the era of “bigger is better” is giving way to one where 
efficiency and sovereignty dominate. If that shift accelerates, the 
advantage will belong not to those who can burn the most compute, but to 
those who can design the most efficient, self-improving, and verifiable 
loops.

The great unbundling is not a single event. It is a structural phase 
change in how knowledge is produced, distributed, and controlled. The book 
is becoming a service; the nation is becoming a loop. And the architecture 
that makes both transformations possible is the same: a model that never 
stops learning.
