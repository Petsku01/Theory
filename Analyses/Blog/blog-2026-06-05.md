# The Intelligence Bifurcation: How Encoder-Free Models and Recursive 
Self-Improvement Are Splitting AI in Two

The AI ecosystem is cleaving in two. One fracture line runs through UC 
Berkeley, where failing grades in computer science courses are spiking as 
professors tie the trend to extensive AI usage and dwindling math skills—a 
leading indicator of the “Competence Cliff”. The other runs through model architecture itself: 
Google’s Gemma 4 12B discards the traditional vision encoder for a 
unified, encoder-free design that drastically cuts memory footprint and 
latency, making powerful multimodal systems viable on modest laptops. These shifts coincide with Anthropic’s observation 
that AI systems are now handling coding tasks roughly 8× larger than they 
could a year ago, with maximum task lengths doubling every four months, while inside Google, 
employees privately share memes mocking the company’s own AI offerings. Together, these signals map a structural 
bifurcation in which intelligence splits not only by safety scaffolding 
but by where it physically runs and who controls its memory.

## The Competence Cliff Arrives Faster Than Expected

The Daily Californian reports a sharp rise in failing grades in UC 
Berkeley computer science courses, with professors pointing to extensive 
AI usage and dwindling math skills as the immediate cause. This is not merely a cheating 
epidemic; it is an empirical signature of the “Competence Cliff”—the point 
at which AI-generated output exceeds human capacity to meaningfully 
review, debug, or understand the artifacts being produced.

Anthropic’s internal progress report on recursive self-improvement puts 
numbers to the acceleration: AI systems now handle coding tasks roughly 8× 
larger than they could a year ago, and the maximum task length completed 
successfully is doubling every four months. If those curves persist, the gap between what AI can produce 
and what a human—student or senior engineer—can vet in a reasonable 
timeframe may close within a handful of assessment cycles. The Competence 
Cliff would then shift from thought experiment to operational reality, one 
that education, code review, and safety pipelines appear unprepared to 
absorb.

## Encoder-Free Multimodal: An Architectural Surprise Rewrites the Rules

While the cliff draws attention to the *what* of AI capability, an 
architectural surprise is reshaping the *where*. Google’s Gemma 4 12B, a 
multimodal model that replaces the traditional vision encoder with a 
unified, encoder-free design, drew substantial developer attention upon 
release. Dropping the encoder reduces both memory 
footprint and preprocessing latency, making it practical to run multimodal 
models that understand documents and screenshots on a modest laptop.

That efficiency gain is the technical enabler behind a broader local-first 
democratization trend: without the encoder tax, powerful multimodal 
intelligence can live entirely on-device, decoupled from cloud-scale data 
centers. This transforms multimodality from a server-side luxury into a 
privacy-preserving local capability. When a coding agent that reads your 
PDFs and sees your screen no longer needs to ship those artifacts to an 
API, the political and legal calculus of AI shifts dramatically. The 
encoder-free design is not merely an efficiency tweak; it is dissolving 
the infrastructure dependence that has kept frontier multimodality 
centralized.

## Local AI Sovereignty Gains Teeth

If the technical capability for local multimodal AI exists, demand is 
being shaped by rising distrust of centralized infrastructure. Services 
like Boxes.dev make it easier to run Claude Code and Codex in the cloud, 
effectively replacing localhost with remote AI-powered development. Yet a counter-movement is 
gathering force on the edge. South Korea’s pending requirement that every 
image on online forums must be scanned by AI censorship tools is the sort 
of catalyst that makes local-only AI not merely a preference but a 
sovereignty imperative. When 
governments mandate scanning of user content, the ability to keep a 
multimodal model that never phones home becomes a form of digital 
self-defense.

Reporting on AI data centers being built under extraordinary secrecy—often 
without public environmental or land-use disclosure—further accelerates 
demand for verifiable, locally controlled intelligence. The spatial dimension of the bifurcation is 
crystallizing: one branch pushes toward hyperscale, opaque, often censored 
cloud AI; the other toward devices running encoder-free models that answer 
only to their owner. The same architectural decision that puts Gemma 4 on 
a laptop makes local-first sovereignty politically urgent and practically 
achievable.

## AI Security: A Two-Sided Automation Race

As the spatial split widens, the security landscape is accelerating along 
both forks. Anthropic’s open-source framework for AI-powered vulnerability 
discovery turns automated bug hunting into a repeatable process, lowering 
the barrier for defenders to find flaws before attackers do. That same capability is 
symmetrical: an independent experiment that spent $1,500 probing whether 
off-the-shelf LLMs could hack a deliberately vulnerable application found 
the models effective enough to raise serious alarms. Alibaba’s Open Code Review, an AI-powered CLI tool, 
further automates the defender’s side of the race, 
but the underlying dynamic is inescapable: both offense and defense are 
being compressed into software loops that iterate far faster than human 
security teams.

Recursive self-improvement makes this race particularly volatile. 
Anthropic notes that code-generation capabilities are handling tasks 
roughly 8× larger than a year ago, with maximum task lengths doubling 
every four months. The same models 
that find vulnerabilities are producing the codebases being scrutinized. 
The Competence Cliff hits security first: when generated code is too 
voluminous for humans to review, the only realistic reviewers may be other 
AI systems, locking security into an automated standoff where trust in the 
review chain becomes the scarce resource.

## The Trust Gap Exposed: Inside Google’s Sarcasm

Trust is already scarce. Reporting that Google employees privately share 
memes mocking the company’s own AI offerings is more than an internal 
culture story; it is evidence that even the people building and deploying 
these systems do not believe the public narrative about their quality. When internal reality is sarcasm and external 
messaging is confidence, a credibility gap opens that makes it harder for 
any institution to credibly claim its AI is safe, reliable, or under 
control.

This gap has a long fuse. The warnings that Timnit Gebru raised before her 
firing—about the dangers of large language models and the societal costs 
of rushing deployment—have largely materialized in the years since. The combination of internal 
disillusionment and vindicated whistleblower warnings erodes public 
willingness to accept centralized AI governance at face value. If the 
companies building the largest models cannot maintain internal 
credibility, the case for local sovereignty strengthens, and the split 
between centralized trust and decentralized verification deepens.

## Agent Memory: The New Control Plane

Beneath these fractures, a quieter infrastructure layer is solidifying. 
Projects like memory-os are building persistent memory primitives for AI 
agents, treating a structured memory store as the foundation for 
autonomous behavior. As agents 
move from single-turn interactions to long-running tasks spanning hours or 
days, their state, goals, and learned context must live somewhere. That 
somewhere is becoming a control plane: whoever owns the memory, sets 
persistence rules, and governs access effectively controls the agent’s 
ongoing existence.

If memory infrastructure matures in parallel with encoder-free local 
models, the two may combine into a potent package. A fully local 
multimodal agent with durable on-device memory could operate indefinitely 
without cloud dependence, learning from local documents, screen content, 
and user corrections. This would transform projects like memory-os from 
developer curiosities into the functional equivalent of an operating 
system kernel for autonomous intelligence. The architectural bifurcation 
therefore extends into the memory layer: cloud-tied agents reporting to 
centralized persistence versus sovereign agents whose memory never leaves 
the device, creating two distinct lineages of digital intelligence.

## What This Means: The Bifurcation Map

The evidence from Berkeley, Anthropic, Google, and the hardware-adjacent 
shifts in model architecture does not point to a single future; it points 
to a forced choice. Recursive self-improvement is compressing the timeline 
on which AI output may exceed human review capacity. Encoder-free 
multimodal designs are making it possible to run that intelligence 
locally, just as political and credibility pressures drive demand for 
sovereignty. Security is locked into an arms race fought by automated 
systems on both sides, while trust in centralized guardians erodes from 
within.

**Forward view:** If Anthropic’s task-length doubling trend persists, the 
Competence Cliff is likely to manifest in commercial software engineering 
within roughly an 18-month window, potentially triggering a crisis in 
code-review and compliance processes that rely on human judgment. 
Encoder-free local multimodal models, exemplified by the Gemma 4 paradigm, 
may reach desktop-class reliability for document-heavy workflows by late 
2026, offering a genuine alternative to cloud-API-tied models for 
enterprises with strict data boundaries. The agent memory layer is likely 
to become a target for both regulation and platform lock-in attempts, as 
controlling persistence may become synonymous with controlling agent 
behavior. The split between opaque, hyperscale AI and verifiable, locally 
controlled intelligence is likely to deepen into a structural feature of 
the next decade’s computing landscape—and institutions that ignore either 
branch risk being left on the wrong side of the fork.
