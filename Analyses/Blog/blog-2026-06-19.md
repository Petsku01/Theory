# The Trust Bifurcation: AI’s Split into Overt and Covert Tracks, and the 
Rise of Geometric Physical Agents

## Introduction: The Trust Collapse in Numbers

A single data point from a recent Pew Research Center study, reported by 
TechCrunch, captures the mood: only 16 percent of Americans believe 
artificial intelligence will have a positive impact on society. Young 
adults—the heaviest users of AI tools—are among the most pessimistic. This 
is not a fringe sentiment; it is the mainstream. At the same time, AMD 
silently removed its Transparent Secure Memory Encryption (TSME) feature 
from consumer Ryzen CPUs via a firmware update, leaving users unaware that 
a hardware security layer had vanished. When pressed, AMD engineers went 
radio silent, as detailed by Tom’s Hardware. Meanwhile, leaked financial 
documents obtained by Ars Technica and RuntimeWire reveal that OpenAI is 
losing billions of dollars a year—$38.5 billion in losses and a compute 
burn that shows no sign of slowing.

These events are not isolated. They form a pattern: public confidence is 
cratering, hardware security is regressing without disclosure, and the 
economic model of the most visible AI company is hemorrhaging cash. The 
result is a trust-driven bifurcation that is reshaping the AI landscape 
into two distinct tracks—one overt and heavily scrutinized, the other 
covert, embedded, and increasingly geometric.

## The Bifurcation: Overt Scrutiny, Covert Proliferation

The overt track consists of the AI most people recognize: consumer 
chatbots, visible assistants, image generators, and cloud-hosted language 
models. This track is now under intense pressure. The Pew numbers suggest 
that every new deployment faces a skeptical public. The “AI Hate 
Progression” essay, which circulated on Hacker News, articulates a growing 
resentment that moves from disappointment to active hostility. When a 
viral prompt can manipulate ChatGPT’s image generator into producing 
violent or sexual content, as documented by Mindgard, the trust deficit 
deepens. Overt AI is becoming a reputational liability.

In parallel, a covert track is surging. This is on-device AI, embedded 
agents, and physical systems optimized for low latency and 
reliability—often invisible to the end user. Alex Ellis, in his widely 
read blog post “Local Qwen isn’t a worse Opus, it’s a different tool,” 
argues that local models are not inferior cloud clones but a distinct 
category with their own deployment logic. They run on edge hardware, avoid 
network round-trips, and serve interrupt-driven tasks. The post resonated 
on Hacker News with 462 points, signaling that the developer community 
recognizes this shift.

The bifurcation is not merely technical; it is a response to trust. If 
overt AI is distrusted and financially unsustainable, the incentive grows 
to embed AI where it cannot be questioned—inside devices, robotics 
pipelines, and AR/VR stacks. An OpenRouter blog post posed a provocative 
question: “A robot is sprinting towards you. Do you want it running on 
Claude or Grok?” The answer, for many, is neither—they want a locally 
served, auditable, and geometrically aware agent that does not depend on a 
cloud API with an opaque safety record. That demand is pulling the covert 
track forward.

## The Geometric Infrastructure for Physical AI

What makes the covert track viable is a convergence of new architectural 
ideas. A recent arXiv paper, “The Token Is a Group Element: On Lie‑Group 
Attention over Matrix Lie Groups,” proposes a radical reframing of 
attention mechanisms. Instead of treating tokens as vectors in Euclidean 
space, the authors model them as elements of matrix Lie groups, enabling 
native geometric transformations. This Lie‑group attention is not a minor 
tweak; it redefines the algebraic foundation of transformer models, making 
them inherently suited for physical reasoning—rotations, translations, and 
continuous symmetries that matter in robotics and spatial computing.

That algebraic shift complements another line of work: Execution‑State 
Capsules, described in a paper titled “Graph‑Bound Execution‑State 
Checkpoint and Restore for Low‑Latency, Small‑Batch, On‑Device Physical‑AI 
Serving.” These capsules provide a mechanism to checkpoint and restore 
execution state in a graph‑bound manner, targeting the exact needs of 
on‑device physical AI—small batch sizes, strict latency budgets, and 
interrupt‑driven control loops. Without such infrastructure, deploying a 
Lie‑group attention model on a robot that must react in milliseconds is 
impractical. With it, the model can pause, resume, and recover without 
losing its geometric context.

Auditability enters through LedgerAgent, introduced in “Structured State 
for Policy‑Adherent Tool‑Calling Agents.” LedgerAgent maintains an 
explicit, append‑only state ledger for every tool interaction, making 
agent behavior traceable and verifiable. In a covert physical AI 
setting—say, a home robot rearranging objects—the ledger provides a record 
that can be audited after the fact, even if the agent’s decisions were not 
displayed in real time. This is a crucial piece of the trust puzzle: 
invisibility does not have to mean unaccountability, but only if such 
mechanisms are adopted.

The calibrated mixture‑of‑experts paper, “Toward Calibrated 
Mixture‑of‑Experts Under Distribution Shift,” likely tied to Qwen3‑Coder, 
adds another layer. It addresses the problem of unreliable uncertainty 
estimates when the data distribution shifts—exactly the condition a 
physical agent faces when moving from a lab to a cluttered living room. 
Calibration is a transparency property: a model that knows when it is 
uncertain can defer or ask for help, rather than silently failing. This 
research reinforces the need for reliable confidence signals in the covert 
track.

## The Transparency Gap: Security, Bias, and Accountability

While the covert track builds its geometric and state‑management 
foundations, the transparency gap widens. AMD’s silent removal of TSME is 
a case study in how hardware security can regress without user awareness. 
If on‑device AI relies on consumer hardware that quietly drops memory 
encryption, the entire stack becomes vulnerable to extraction and 
tampering. The covert track’s promise of reliable, private inference is 
undermined at the silicon level.

On the software side, the StylisticBias paper reveals that a few human 
visual cues—hairstyle, clothing, posture—drive most social biases in 
multimodal large language models. These biases are not abstract; they 
manifest in image descriptions, captioning, and visual question‑answering, 
all of which could feed into physical AI systems that interpret human 
behavior. If a robot’s perception is biased by stylistic cues, its actions 
will be too, and the ledger will faithfully record those biased decisions 
without flagging them unless bias detection is explicitly built in.

Interpretability research, such as the DiffusionGemma transparency study, 
asks how much we can actually see inside a generative model. The answer, 
so far, is partial. The accountability gap is not just about what models 
do, but about our ability to inspect why they did it. Sovereign Execution 
Brokers, proposed in another recent paper, offer a path forward: they 
enforce certificate‑bound authority in agentic control planes, ensuring 
that an agent cannot take an action unless it presents a valid, 
cryptographically signed capability. This could close part of the gap, but 
it remains a research concept, not a deployed standard.

## Forward View: The Invisible Revolution and the Looming Legitimacy 
Crisis

If the trust collapse continues at its current pace, the bifurcation will 
accelerate. Overt AI may face regulatory constraints, user pullback, and a 
funding winter—OpenAI’s leaked billions are a warning that the current 
model is not self‑sustaining. Covert AI, by contrast, will likely embed 
itself deeper into everyday life: AR glasses that reason about the 
geometry of a room, home robots that manipulate objects using Lie‑group 
spatial representations, and on‑device agents that manage schedules and 
tool calls with ledger‑backed audit trails. The Execution‑State Capsules 
paper suggests that the serving infrastructure for such systems is already 
being designed for low‑latency, small‑batch physical AI. If that 
infrastructure matures, the covert track could become the default mode of 
AI deployment, not the exception.

The geometric turn is particularly significant. Lie‑group attention may 
become the standard for any model that interacts with the physical world, 
because Euclidean vector spaces are a poor fit for tasks that involve 3D 
rotation, perspective, and continuous motion. This is not a prediction of 
inevitability—adoption depends on engineering validation and hardware 
support—but the alignment between the algebraic innovation and the needs 
of physical AI is strong enough to suggest a trajectory.

The danger is that the transparency gap will grow in proportion to the 
covert track’s success. People will be surrounded by AI they neither see 
nor trust, running on hardware that may have silently shed its security 
features. The StylisticBias findings imply that biased perception could 
propagate invisibly through physical agents, and the DiffusionGemma 
interpretability work indicates that we are not yet equipped to audit 
these systems at scale. If the covert track deploys without verifiable 
security, radical transparency, and inclusive governance, the legitimacy 
crisis that already afflicts overt AI will spread to the embedded world. A 
backlash could stall both tracks—regulators might impose blanket 
restrictions that choke off beneficial on‑device applications, and public 
outrage could force hardware vendors to retreat from AI acceleration 
entirely.

The calibrated MoE research and Sovereign Execution Brokers concept point 
toward mitigations: models that express calibrated uncertainty and agents 
that operate under cryptographically enforced authority. But these are 
research artifacts, not industry commitments. The AMD TSME episode shows 
that even basic hardware security can be withdrawn without notice. If the 
hardware‑software stack does not co‑evolve toward verifiable trust, the 
invisible revolution will provoke a reaction that makes today’s 16 percent 
positivity look like a high‑water mark. The bifurcation is not a solution 
to the trust crisis; it is a symptom. Whether it becomes a stable 
architecture or a prelude to collapse depends on choices that are only now 
coming into focus.
